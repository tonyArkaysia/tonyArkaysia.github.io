Legal Context

Warranty of Habitability

The landlords have breached the implied warranty of habitability by failing to address severe noise and vibration issues that render the premises unlivable. The continuous low-frequency noise, structural resonance, and resulting health impacts constitute a fundamental breach of the landlord's duty to maintain habitable premises.

Retaliatory Eviction (RPL Â§223-b)

The eviction notice served on August 22, 2025, was drafted on August 13, 2025â€”the day after the first court appearance was adjourned for due process. This timing, combined with the lack of any substantive court findings or orders, demonstrates clear retaliation for the tenant's good faith complaints and HPD case filing. The eviction notice was prepared before any meaningful court discussion could occur, indicating retaliatory intent.

Good Cause Eviction (RPL Â§231-c)

Under New York's Good Cause Eviction Law, landlords must demonstrate good cause for eviction. In this case, the eviction notice was issued in direct response to the tenant's protected activity of reporting violations and seeking relief through housing court. No legitimate cause has been demonstrated, and the timing strongly suggests retaliation.

HPD Class B Violation Status

Multiple HPD Class B violations were open and pending at the time the eviction notice was issued. These violations, combined with the ongoing noise and habitability issues, demonstrate a pattern of neglect and failure to maintain the premises in habitable condition. View violations on HPD Online.

Constructive Eviction

The combination of unaddressed noise violations, structural issues, health impacts, and retaliatory eviction efforts constitutes constructive eviction. The premises have been rendered effectively unlivable, and the landlord's actions have forced the tenant to seek relief while simultaneously threatening their tenancy.

Technical Testing Protocol & Court Petition Framework

Strategic Legal Approach - Phased Petition

This comprehensive technical testing protocol outlines a three-phase legal strategy for establishing evidence of noise violations and structural defects contributing to habitability issues. The approach emphasizes independent acoustical testing, baseline evidence gathering, and evidence-based court orders against all responsible parties.

Phase 1: Petition landlord for immediate access to conduct independent acoustical testing while all noise sources (church, bar, landlord equipment) operate normally. Preserve tenancy during investigation.

Phase 2: Gather baseline evidence through continuous monitoring (unannounced to church/bar). Document violations, peak events, and multi-source contributions with spectral analysis.

Phase 3: Use Phase 2 evidence to compel church and bar participation in controlled testing, equipment documentation, and joint remediation plan with all responsible parties.
